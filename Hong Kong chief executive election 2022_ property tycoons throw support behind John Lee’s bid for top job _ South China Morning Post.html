Hong Kong chief executive election 2022: property tycoons throw support behind John Lee’s bid for top job

  • Sun Hung Kai Properties chairman Raymond Kwok, New World Development CEO Adrian Cheng among top land developers to endorse chief executive hopeful John Lee
  • Standing committee delegate Tam Yiu-chung declined to say what role he would play in the campaign, but confirmed he supported Lee

Topic |   Hong Kong chief executive election 2022

Several of Hong Kong’s top property tycoons threw their support one after another behind chief executive hopeful John Lee Ka-chiu, as he tapped the sole local delegate to the country’s top legislative body to lead his election campaign.

Property tycoons who announced their support for Lee on Thursday included Sun Hung Kai Properties chairman Raymond Kwok Ping-luen; Victor Li Tzar-kuoi, the eldest son of billionaire Li Ka-shing and chairman of CK Hutchison Holdings and CK Asset Holdings; New World Development CEO Adrian Cheng Chi-kong and Henderson Land co-chairman Martin Lee Ka-shing.

Wharf Real Estate Investment Company, chaired by Stephen Ng Tin-hoi, also called Lee a suitable candidate “to lead Hong Kong for the new chapter starting, 25 years after the British handover in 1997”.

Sino Group vice-chairman and real estate developer Daryl Ng Win-kong also backed the former No 2 official, joining Lee’s campaign as one of his three election expense agents.

Sources also told the Post that more than 10 representatives from various industries were set to join the team in a bid to show solidarity, with Lee expected to announce his bid for the city’s top job at an online presser at 3pm on Friday.

Tam Yiu-chung, Hong Kong’s sole delegate to the National People’s Congress Standing Committee visited Lee’s campaign headquarters at Central Plaza in Wan Chai on Thursday.

After spending two hours at the office tower, Tam told reporters that he would “support Lee as the next chief executive and recommend him to others”.

A source said Tam was expected to serve as Lee’s campaign office director, and had returned to the city from Shenzhen – where he was preparing for the next standing committee meeting in mid-April – specifically to help the chief executive hopeful with the race.

“As a leader, the most important thing is to know how to deploy talent, and to lead the team to serve Hong Kong well,” Tam said of Lee. “No one knows everything, it’s already enough when he has the heart [to serve] and the capability to lead the team … He is also experienced and has gained the trust of the central government.”

“I also believe he can unite all sectors in society, and will strive hard to boost the city’s economic development, improve people’s livelihood and do the anti-epidemic work well,” Tam added, but refused to comment on whether Lee’s manifesto would focus on national security.

The same source also said that former constitutional and mainland affairs minister Raymond Tam Chi-yuen, now an NPC delegate, would act as deputy director of Lee’s campaign office, while more than 10 representatives from across Hong Kong’s various industries were also set to join the team.

In a reply to the Post, Tam said: “I fully support [Lee] in standing as the next chief executive, I’m willing to do whatever I can to help with the relevant work.”

Tam Yiu-chung, Hong Kong’s sole delegate to the National People’s Congress Standing Committee. Photo: Nora Tam

At least four prominent land developers issued statements on Thursday evening to express their support for Lee, with Sun Hung Kai Properties’ Kwok being the first to endorse the former senior minister.

“I am convinced that Lee has the ability to work for the benefit of Hong Kong’s future, and lead Hong Kong to further integrate into the country’s overall national development,” he said in a statement.

Victor Li of CK Hutchison Holdings and CK Asset Holdings also said he would support Lee.

“John Lee is a suitable choice as the city’s next chief executive, I will nominate him,” Li said on Thursday, a day after he urged the city’s next leader to help improve living conditions and lead Hong Kong to stability and prosperity.

Henderson Land’s Martin Lee called the chief executive hopeful an “experienced, courageous and patriotic candidate”.

“I believe he is a suitable person to lead the Hong Kong government and capable of leading Hong Kong into the future, ensuring the steady implementation of ‘one country, two systems’ and making a contribution to the nation,” he said.

Adrian Cheng of New World Development also praised Lee, citing his role in implementing the national security law as well as Beijing’s electoral overhaul.

“The new chief executive needs to lead the government and society in reviving the economy and improving people’s livelihoods,” he said.

“The new leader also needs to be responsible, courageous and capable of tackling difficulties and uniting Hong Kong. I believe that Lee has these qualities.”

Other backers of Lee include All-China Youth Federation vice-chairman Clarence Leung Wang-ching and Kenneth Fok Kai-kong, the lawmaker for the sports, performing arts, culture and publication sector.

“These are mostly honorary posts to show that Lee has the support of, and would listen to people from all walks of life in society,” the source said, adding that a group of advisers would also be joining the campaign office.

According to documents submitted to election authorities, Lee has already appointed three people to act as his election expense agents. The trio will be authorised to incur any election expenses on behalf of Lee and his team.

The three agents consist of Sino Group’s Daryl Ng; Pauline Ng Man-wah, former secretary general of the Legislative Council; as well as Eliza Chan Ching-har, a lawyer and Hong Kong delegate to the Chinese People’s Political Consultative Conference, the nation’s top advisory body.

The trio also held positions in incumbent leader Carrie Lam Cheng Yuet-ngor’s election campaign five years ago. Chan and Daryl Ng acted as deputy directors of Lam’s team while Pauline Ng served as an election expense agent.

Chief executive candidates are allowed to incur election expenses of up to HK$17.6 million as part of an election bid. During Lee’s campaign, Chan and Pauline Ng will be able to spend up to HK$7 million each, while the maximum amount for Daryl Ng is HK$3.6 million.

The paper also indicated that the signing of the trio’s authorisation documents was witnessed by delegate Tam.

Last week, Lee declared as then chief secretary that any holder of his position could give permission for group gatherings in relation to the election.

But a source said Lee would hold an online press conference to avoid legal challenges as he had resigned from his former post and there was no incumbent office holder who could authorise an in-person event.

Meanwhile, Lam on Thursday declined to say whether she supported Lee as the city’s next leader or if she stood by her remarks last year that his experience in youth and poverty alleviation policies was relatively weak.

“I’ve said that I will not comment on the sixth chief executive election. My only responsibility is to make sure that the poll will be organised smoothly, so I will not answer a series of questions from you,” she said, adding that it was important for the chief executive to be both accountable to the central government as well as locally.

Accompanying Lee to Central Plaza on Thursday were Wong Tsz-yuen, a 34-year-old former Phoenix TV host and Election Committee member, and Joanne Cheung Shui-lin, a public affairs consultant and director of the Hong Kong United Foundation who is closely associated with former city leader Leung Chun-ying.

One local politician said he expected the initial stage of campaigning to mostly comprise events aimed at heavyweights, with public outreach to residents put on hold until after April 21, when social-distancing measures were expected to be relaxed.

Basic Law Committee vice-chairwoman Maria Tam Wai-chu. Photo: Xiaomei Chen

Pro-Beijing sources told the Post on Wednesday that Beijing’s liaison office in Hong Kong had told Election Committee members that Lee would be the only candidate in the race with the central government’s blessing.

On the same day, Basic Law Committee vice-chairwoman Maria Tam Wai-chu told a radio programme that the lack of competition during this year’s election could be an isolated incident as she threw her support behind Lee.

“Of course, it would be good if we have competition,” she said. “But not having it this time doesn’t mean it is going to be permanent.”

A career policeman before he became the city’s security minister known for his stern approach to the 2019 anti-government protests – and then Hong Kong’s no 2 official in June last year – Lee’s lack of experience in handling finance and livelihood issues have been called into question since he announced his leadership bid.

But Maria Tam said the change in administrations would require stability and policy continuity, with senior ministers and members of the Executive Council, the city’s top advisory body, the most suitable successors to Lam.

“The safer Hong Kong is, the more confidence Beijing will have in the city to let it develop on its own,” she said.

Ma Fung-kwok, the leader of Hong Kong’s NPC delegation, noted that Lee had not put public support on the top of his agenda when he previously served as a security official.

But Ma urged Hongkongers not to rule out the former chief minister’s ability to improve and praised Lee for his seriousness.

Tik Chi-yuen, the city’s sole non-establishment lawmaker, said a chief executive should demonstrate both a soft and hard side, with Lee only being known for his tough approach.

The lawmaker said that solving “black violence”, a term used by the pro-establishment camp for the unrest in 2019, was not just a national security issue but required a focus on youth development and other welfare problems.
